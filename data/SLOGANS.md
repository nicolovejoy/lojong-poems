# Lojong Slogans — Annotated for Found Poetry
*59 slogans of Chekawa Yeshe Dorje, organized in 7 Points*  
*Annotations optimized for news resonance scoring*

---

## How to Use This Document

Each slogan has:
- **Gloss** — what the slogan actually means in practice (brief, non-jargon)
- **Core tension** — the paradox or reversal at its heart (this is what makes poems)
- **Themes** — for resonance scoring against news stories
- **Search seeds** — concrete keywords to find stories with structural fit
- **Notes** — observations about poetic potential, difficulty, or unusual angles

The best slogans for this project are ones where the *structure* of a news story mirrors the *structure* of the teaching — not just the subject matter. A story about invisibility doesn't need to be about self-effacement; it needs to *enact* something similar.

## Point One: The Preliminaries

*Foundation practices — awareness of impermanence, preciousness, suffering, and the possibility of liberation.*

---

### 1. First, train in the preliminaries

**Gloss:** Before anything else, ground yourself in four basic recognitions: human life is rare and precious; it ends; suffering is real; liberation is possible.

**Core tension:** The extraordinary is hidden inside the ordinary. What we take for granted is the very thing that could save us.

**Themes:** rarity, attention, groundedness, overlooked value, baseline conditions

**Search seeds:** "overlooked" "taken for granted" "rare" "baseline" "what we ignore" "invisible infrastructure" "fundamental" "preconditions"

**Notes:** Strong candidate. Stories about things we assumed were stable until they weren't — aquifers, coral reefs, languages, small newspapers, certain bird populations. The "preliminaries" angle maps well onto stories about foundations only noticed when they fail.

---

## Point Two: The Main Practice — Bodhicitta

*The heart of lojong: training in both ultimate reality (emptiness) and relative reality (compassion). These slogans address how to hold both simultaneously.*

---

### 2. Regard all dharmas as dreams

**Gloss:** Everything you experience — including your thoughts, emotions, and perceptions — is like a dream: vivid but not solid. This doesn't mean nothing matters; it means our fixation on things being permanent and real as they appear is mistaken.

**Core tension:** Full engagement with something known to be insubstantial. Taking the dream seriously enough to act, while knowing it's a dream.

**Themes:** simulation, illusion, constructed reality, appearance vs. substance, virtual, memory, grief

**Search seeds:** "virtual reality" "deepfake" "false memory" "reconstructed" "simulation" "placebo" "phantom" "hallucination" "artificial" "facade"

**Notes:** High potential. Stories about AI-generated reality, deepfakes, reconstructed faces of missing persons, virtual grief spaces. Also: economic bubbles, potemkin villages, Disneyland-style constructed communities.

---

### 3. Examine the nature of unborn awareness

**Gloss:** Look at your own mind directly. What is consciousness before thought arises? This is a pointing-out instruction — hard to explain, meant to be experienced.

**Core tension:** The observer cannot observe itself without becoming the observed. Consciousness trying to see consciousness.

**Themes:** self-reference, paradox, observation effects, consciousness, origin, before/after

**Search seeds:** "consciousness" "observer effect" "self-referential" "origin" "before birth" "quantum measurement" "introspection limits" "neural correlates"

**Notes:** Harder to find direct news fit. Best angle: scientific attempts to locate or measure consciousness — hard problem of consciousness research, psychedelic studies (Pollan territory), near-death experience research.

---

### 4. Self-liberate even the antidote

**Gloss:** Even the cure becomes a problem if you cling to it. The medicine that heals you can become another form of grasping if you turn it into an identity or ideology.

**Core tension:** The solution that becomes the next problem. Remedies that calcify into dogma.

**Themes:** overcorrection, cure becoming disease, ideology, backlash, antidote toxicity, activism burnout, reform becoming institution

**Search seeds:** "overcorrection" "cure worse than disease" "reform becomes establishment" "antidote" "backlash" "activist burnout" "purity culture" "cancel culture" "the fix" "unintended consequences"

**Notes:** Excellent candidate. Rich news territory: movements that become dogmatic, safety measures that create new dangers, pharmaceutical side effects, addiction recovery that becomes addiction to recovery. The Bulley-style move would be: find a story where the language of remedy is itself the problem.

---

### 5. Rest in the nature of alaya, the essence

**Gloss:** Underneath all mental activity is a ground of open, spacious awareness. Return to that ground. Don't manufacture stillness — discover what's already there.

**Core tension:** The thing you're looking for is where you're looking from.

**Themes:** ground state, stillness, baseline, underneath noise, rest, substrate, foundation beneath activity

**Search seeds:** "dead zones" "silence" "background radiation" "resting state" "default mode" "deep ocean" "bedrock" "substrate" "stillness" "quiet" "pause"

**Notes:** More meditative; harder news fit. Best angle: scientific stories about resting states, default mode network, cosmic background radiation, the "quiet" beneath city noise, silence as endangered resource.

---

### 6. In post-meditation, be a child of illusion

**Gloss:** When not in formal meditation, carry the understanding from practice into everyday life — but lightly, playfully, without forcing it. Engage fully while knowing the dream-like quality of things.

**Core tension:** Participation with lightness. Serious engagement held with a kind of play.

**Themes:** play, lightness, participation, improvisation, engagement without grasping, wonder

**Search seeds:** "play" "improvisation" "lightness" "wonder" "child-like" "beginner's mind" "flow state" "curiosity" "engagement" "presence"

**Notes:** Gentler territory. Stories about adults recovering play, flow states in athletes or artists, research on wonder and curiosity, beginner's mind in expert practitioners.

---

### 7. Sending and taking should be practiced alternately; put them on the breath

**Gloss:** Tonglen practice: breathe in suffering (yours and others'), breathe out relief and goodness. The exchange happens on the breath itself — the most intimate, automatic thing we do.

**Core tension:** The body doing something radical — absorbing pain, releasing goodness — through its most unconscious act.

**Themes:** exchange, absorption, giving, breath, intimacy, bodies, care, transmission, invisible labor

**Search seeds:** "breathing" "ventilator" "caretaker" "hospice" "absorb" "exchange" "filter" "dialysis" "invisible care work" "first responders" "transmission"

**Notes:** Strong candidate. The breath/exchange structure maps onto stories about care workers, dialysis patients, air filtration systems, those who absorb others' suffering professionally. Pandemic-era material especially rich here.

---

### 8. Three objects, three poisons, three roots of virtue

**Gloss:** Friend, enemy, stranger — these trigger desire, aggression, and indifference respectively. Recognize these three reactions; they are also the seeds of the three virtues (generosity, patience, equanimity).

**Core tension:** The same relationship that produces poison produces virtue, depending on what you do with it.

**Themes:** classification, categories, in-group/out-group, threat response, tribalism, indifference to strangers

**Search seeds:** "in-group" "out-group" "tribalism" "stranger" "indifference" "bystander" "us vs. them" "enemy image" "dehumanization" "empathy gap"

**Notes:** Directly relevant to social division stories. Especially: research on bystander effect, indifference to distant suffering, moral circle expansion, dehumanization studies.

---

### 9. In all activities, train with slogans

**Gloss:** Bring the practice into everything — eating, working, conflict, pleasure. The slogans are not retreat tools but life tools.

**Core tension:** The mundane as the site of transformation. No separation between practice and ordinary life.

**Themes:** integration, everyday, mundane, habit, embodied practice, ordinary as sacred

**Search seeds:** "habit" "everyday practice" "embodied" "mundane" "ordinary" "ritual" "integration" "daily life"

**Notes:** Meta-slogan, harder to use directly. Possible angle: stories about micro-practices, habit formation science, or communities that integrate unusual ethics into daily routine.

---

### 10. Begin the sequence of sending and taking with yourself

**Gloss:** Start tonglen with your own suffering. You can't give what you don't have. Compassion for yourself is the foundation for compassion for others — not selfishness.

**Core tension:** Self-care as radical act, not indulgence. The necessity of receiving before you can give.

**Themes:** self-compassion, inner resource, receiving, burnout, starting point, self as other

**Search seeds:** "caregiver burnout" "compassion fatigue" "self-care" "oxygen mask" "systemic" "starting with oneself" "internal resource" "self-compassion research"

**Notes:** Good fit with caregiver burnout stories, healthcare worker mental health, research on self-compassion vs. self-criticism.

## Point Three: Transforming Adversity into the Path

*These slogans address how to work with difficulty, pain, and bad circumstances — not avoiding them but using them.*

---

### 11. When the world is filled with evil, transform all mishaps into the path of bodhi

**Gloss:** When everything is going wrong — corruption, injustice, personal disaster — use the difficulty itself as fuel for awakening. Don't wait for good circumstances.

**Core tension:** The worst conditions as the most fertile ground. Catastrophe as curriculum.

**Themes:** crisis, transformation, adversity, corruption, systemic failure, resilience, turning point

**Search seeds:** "crisis" "disaster" "collapse" "turning point" "resilience" "adversity" "unlikely recovery" "silver lining" "phoenix" "transformation through difficulty"

**Notes:** Very broad — needs specificity in story selection. Best angle: stories where terrible circumstances produced unexpected growth or insight that wouldn't have happened otherwise. Not inspirational cliché — more structural: what the catastrophe made possible.

---

### 12. Drive all blames into one

**Gloss:** Stop distributing blame outward. Take full responsibility — not from self-punishment, but as a practice of stopping the spiral of blame-shifting that perpetuates suffering.

**Core tension:** Radical responsibility that is not self-flagellation. Owning what happens not because you caused it but as a practice of stopping the blame cycle.

**Themes:** accountability, responsibility, blame culture, scapegoating, systemic blame-shifting, collective vs. individual responsibility

**Search seeds:** "blame" "accountability" "scapegoat" "responsibility" "deflection" "collective guilt" "who's responsible" "systemic blame" "passing the buck"

**Notes:** Rich territory. Corporate blame-shifting, political scapegoating, systemic vs. individual responsibility debates (e.g., opioid crisis, climate change). The structural move: find a story where blame circulates endlessly and nothing is ever resolved.

---

### 13. Be grateful to everyone

**Gloss:** Every difficult person, every obstacle, every irritant is your teacher. Gratitude extends even to enemies because they provide the friction that develops patience and insight.

**Core tension:** Gratitude for what you didn't want. The adversary as benefactor.

**Themes:** unexpected gratitude, adversary as teacher, friction, irritant, difficult relationships, perspective shift

**Search seeds:** "unexpected gratitude" "difficult colleague" "adversary" "friction" "obstacle" "changed my mind" "perspective shift" "thanks to" "because of"

**Notes:** Best angle: scientific or social research on how opposition strengthens — immune systems needing exposure, ideas needing critique, materials needing stress-testing. Or stories of people who publicly thanked those who harmed them.

---

### 14. Seeing confusion as the four kayas is unsurpassable shunyata protection

**Gloss:** Technical slogan: when confusion arises, recognize it as the display of awakened mind in four aspects (dharmakaya, sambhogakaya, nirmanakaya, svabhavikakaya). Confusion itself is the path.

**Core tension:** What appears as obstacle is actually the ground of liberation — but only if you have the view to recognize it.

**Themes:** confusion as resource, disorientation, threshold states, liminality, apparent failure as hidden structure

**Search seeds:** "confusion" "disorientation" "threshold" "liminal" "between states" "transition" "disorder" "apparent chaos" "hidden structure"

**Notes:** Technically dense; harder to use directly. Best angle: stories about liminal states that turned out to be formative — refugee experiences, transition zones in ecology (edge effects), states between sleep and waking.

---

### 15. Four practices are the best of methods

**Gloss:** Accumulate merit, purify obscurations, offer to the protectors, make offerings to the dharmapalas. Practical: do good, clean up your messes, respect what protects the teachings, honor the fierce protectors.

**Core tension:** The ordinary housekeeping of spiritual life. Maintenance as practice.

**Themes:** maintenance, upkeep, ordinary care, invisible labor, infrastructure, protection, accumulated small acts

**Search seeds:** "maintenance" "upkeep" "infrastructure" "invisible labor" "accumulated" "small acts" "protection" "stewardship" "caretaking systems"

**Notes:** Good angle: stories about maintenance workers, infrastructure repair, the unsung labor of keeping things going. The spiritual dimension: what protects a culture or ecosystem without being noticed.

---

### 16. Whatever you meet unexpectedly, join with meditation

**Gloss:** When surprised — by joy, shock, grief, beauty — bring that energy directly into practice rather than reacting habitually. The unexpected is an invitation.

**Core tension:** The unrehearsed moment as the real test. Improvisation as the deepest practice.

**Themes:** surprise, shock, improvisation, unexpected encounter, interruption, disruption

**Search seeds:** "unexpected" "surprise" "disruption" "improvisation" "accident" "chance encounter" "serendipity" "shock" "sudden"

**Notes:** Strong candidate. Stories about accidental discoveries (scientific, artistic, personal), chance encounters with consequences, disruptions that opened something. The Bulley story is essentially this — Karen Osborn was looking for crabs.

## Point Four: Showing the Utilization of Practice in One's Whole Life

*How the practice integrates across an entire life — especially at its end.*

---

### 17. Practice the five strengths, the condensed heart instructions

**Gloss:** Five qualities for sustained practice: strong intention, familiarization (keep returning), positive seed (white seed, virtue), reproach (honest self-confrontation), aspiration. These are also the instructions for dying well.

**Core tension:** The practice of a lifetime condensed into something that can be held in a dying moment.

**Themes:** distillation, essence, dying, what matters, priorities, condensed wisdom, five-fold structure

**Search seeds:** "deathbed" "what matters" "dying" "priorities" "distillation" "essence" "last words" "hospice" "what remains" "condensed"

**Notes:** Rich territory around dying: hospice care, end-of-life conversations, what people regret, what dying teaches the living. Also: stories about compression — what survives catastrophe, what gets carried forward.

---

### 18. The Mahayana instruction for ejection of consciousness at death is the five strengths: how you conduct yourself is important

**Gloss:** At death, the same five strengths apply. How you die — your state of mind, your relationship to what's happening — matters enormously. "How you conduct yourself" is the heart of it.

**Core tension:** Death as the final exam that reveals whether the practice was real.

**Themes:** dying, conduct, final moments, state of mind, integrity, how we behave when it matters most

**Search seeds:** "dying" "final moments" "integrity" "conduct" "hospice" "last hours" "how we die" "deathbed" "consciousness at death" "peaceful death"

**Notes:** Similar to 17. May be better paired with 17 for a longer poem. Stories about death doulas, hospice volunteers, conversations at end of life.

## Point Five: Evaluation of Mind Training

*How do you know if the practice is working?*

---

### 19. All dharma agrees at one point

**Gloss:** All genuine spiritual teachings, despite their differences, point to one thing: reducing ego-clinging. If your practice isn't reducing selfishness and increasing compassion, something is off.

**Core tension:** The convergence beneath apparent diversity. What looks different on the surface shares a root.

**Themes:** convergence, common ground, beneath differences, root cause, shared foundation, unity beneath diversity

**Search seeds:** "convergence" "common ground" "beneath differences" "shared root" "unity" "different paths same destination" "underlying" "cross-cultural" "universal"

**Notes:** Good angle: scientific stories about convergent evolution (like the ultra-black fish), cross-cultural research showing similar ethics across traditions, stories about unexpected agreement between adversaries.

---

### 20. Of the two witnesses, hold the principal one

**Gloss:** Others can witness your behavior, but only you know your own mind. The inner witness — your own honest self-knowledge — is primary.

**Core tension:** The private truth versus the public performance. What only you know.

**Themes:** inner knowledge, privacy, self-deception, public vs. private, hidden life, integrity without audience

**Search seeds:** "private" "hidden" "what only I know" "self-deception" "integrity" "inner life" "secret" "performance" "public vs. private" "conscience"

**Notes:** Good fit with stories about private vs. public self — whistleblowers, confessors, diarists, those who maintained integrity with no audience. Also: research on self-deception, motivated reasoning.

---

### 21. Always maintain only a joyful mind

**Gloss:** Not toxic positivity — genuine joy arising from the recognition that even difficulty is workable, even poison is medicine. Joy as a ground state, not a feeling to manufacture.

**Core tension:** Joy that doesn't require good circumstances. Happiness that is a practice, not a result.

**Themes:** equanimity, joy, ground state, happiness research, unconditional, not mood-dependent, resilience

**Search seeds:** "equanimity" "joy" "happiness research" "resilience" "not mood-dependent" "unconditional" "baseline happiness" "joy despite" "contentment" "flourishing"

**Notes:** Risk of sentimentality. Best angle: scientific research on happiness (hedonic adaptation, setpoint theory), stories about people who maintained equanimity through extreme adversity — not inspirationally, but structurally.

---

### 22. If you can practice even when distracted, you are well trained

**Gloss:** The real test of practice is not how you do in ideal conditions but how you do when distracted, stressed, overwhelmed, half-asleep. Practice under pressure is the measure.

**Core tension:** Competence revealed under duress. The gap between rehearsal and reality.

**Themes:** distraction, testing, pressure, real conditions vs. ideal conditions, performance under stress, automaticity

**Search seeds:** "under pressure" "distracted" "real conditions" "simulation" "emergency response" "automaticity" "practice under stress" "imperfect conditions" "resilience"

**Notes:** Good fit with stories about emergency responders, pilots, surgeons — where training is tested in chaotic conditions. Also: research on automaticity, skill under stress, what holds when everything else falls away.

---

## Point Six: Disciplines of Mind Training

*Practical guidelines for how to actually practice in daily life.*

---

### 23. Always abide by the three basic principles

**Gloss:** Honor your commitments; don't make your practice about display; be patient — don't rush toward dramatic results.

**Core tension:** Quiet consistency over dramatic gesture. The anti-heroic practice.

**Themes:** consistency, commitment, quiet practice, display vs. substance, patience, anti-heroic

**Search seeds:** "consistency" "quiet work" "commitment" "display" "performative" "substance over style" "long game" "patience" "steady" "undramatic"

**Notes:** Good angle: stories about people doing important work without recognition, institutional commitment over decades, long-term projects with no immediate payoff.

---

### 24. Change your attitude, but remain natural

**Gloss:** Inner transformation should show up in how you relate to life — but not as performance or affectation. The change is real when it becomes natural, not when it's effortful display.

**Core tension:** Authentic transformation vs. performed virtue. When the practice becomes invisible because it's genuine.

**Themes:** authenticity, transformation, naturalness, performance, genuine vs. affected, embodied change

**Search seeds:** "authentic" "genuine" "performance" "affected" "natural" "embodied" "transformation" "spontaneous" "effortless" "second nature"

**Notes:** Interesting angle: stories about people whose values became invisible because they were so deeply integrated — rather than announced. Also: research on habit formation, when behavior becomes automatic.

---

### 25. Don't talk about injured limbs

**Gloss:** Don't highlight others' weaknesses or disabilities — physical, moral, psychological — even in practice of pointing out confusion. Don't exploit vulnerability.

**Core tension:** Silence as protection. What you choose not to say.

**Themes:** silence, protection, vulnerability, exploitation, discretion, what we don't say, kindness through restraint

**Search seeds:** "exploitation" "vulnerability" "disability" "exposing weakness" "silence" "discretion" "protection" "what we don't say" "restraint"

**Notes:** Interesting angle: journalism ethics around when not to publish, protection of vulnerable sources, the decision to withhold information. Also: disability studies, representation, the gaze.

---

### 26. Don't ponder others

**Gloss:** Don't analyze and judge others' faults and shortcomings. It's a waste of the mind that could be turned toward your own practice.

**Core tension:** The intelligence that could be used for growth being spent on gossip and judgment.

**Themes:** judgment, gossip, analysis of others, wasted attention, social media, outrage, surveillance culture

**Search seeds:** "gossip" "judgment" "outrage culture" "surveillance" "judgment" "social media" "attention economy" "judgment" "commentary" "punditry"

**Notes:** Very contemporary relevance. Stories about attention economy, outrage as product, the industry of judging others. Also: research on what happens to wellbeing when we spend time analyzing others vs. self-reflection.

---

### 27. Work with the greatest defilements first

**Gloss:** Go directly to your biggest problem — your most destructive habit, your deepest confusion. Don't start with the easy stuff.

**Core tension:** Tackling the hardest thing first as the most efficient path. The wisdom of going to the source.

**Themes:** root cause, primary problem, avoidance, tackling the hardest, source vs. symptom, intervention

**Search seeds:** "root cause" "source" "primary" "hardest first" "avoidance" "symptom" "intervention" "tackle" "confront" "deepest problem"

**Notes:** Good fit with public health stories about treating root causes vs. symptoms, addiction treatment approaches, systemic vs. surface-level interventions.

---

### 28. Abandon any hope of fruition

**Gloss:** Practice without expecting a payoff. The moment you start practicing to get something — calm, wisdom, recognition — the practice is corrupted.

**Core tension:** Action without result-orientation. Means without ends. The paradox that surrender of outcome is the only path to the outcome.

**Themes:** expectation, outcome-independence, process vs. result, fruition, means and ends, surrender, non-attachment to result

**Search seeds:** "outcome-independent" "process" "non-attachment" "unexpected result" "gave up" "stopped trying" "serendipity" "abandoned" "letting go" "unintended"

**Notes:** Strong candidate. Stories about discoveries made when the goal was abandoned, recoveries that happened when people stopped trying to recover, art made without audience. Research on intrinsic vs. extrinsic motivation.

---

### 29. Abandon poisonous food

**Gloss:** Don't consume things that increase ego-clinging, aggression, or confusion — whether that's certain media, conversations, substances, or thought patterns. Discernment about what you take in.

**Core tension:** What nourishes vs. what poisons, sometimes wearing the same mask.

**Themes:** consumption, nourishment vs. poison, media diet, discernment, what we take in, toxicity, subtle harm

**Search seeds:** "media consumption" "toxic" "social media harm" "information diet" "outrage" "what we consume" "subtle harm" "nourishment" "discernment" "intake"

**Notes:** Very contemporary. Stories about social media's psychological effects, information overload, research on news consumption and mental health, the attention economy as food system.

---

### 30. Don't be so predictable

**Gloss:** Don't hold grudges — don't nurse wrongs, waiting for the right moment to get even. The person who wronged you last year doesn't need to be confronted about it now.

**Core tension:** The release of the script you'd prepared. Letting the grudge expire.

**Themes:** grudge, resentment, score-keeping, letting go, unpredictability, release, the long memory

**Search seeds:** "grudge" "resentment" "score-keeping" "letting go" "forgiveness" "unexpected release" "dropped charges" "amnesty" "moving on"

**Notes:** Good fit with stories about unexpected forgiveness, dropped feuds, nations releasing historical grievances, the science of resentment and health.

---

### 31. Don't malign others

**Gloss:** Don't disparage or demean others — especially spiritual teachers, institutions, or people you find difficult. The harm it does to your own mind is greater than any satisfaction.

**Core tension:** What we say about others says more about us. The wound of contempt falls on both parties.

**Themes:** contempt, disparagement, speech, harm of contempt, reputation, what we say, criticism

**Search seeds:** "contempt" "disparagement" "malign" "reputation damage" "speech" "slander" "online harassment" "contempt research" "what we say about others"

**Notes:** Research on contempt (Gottman's work on relationships), the psychology of contempt, online harassment campaigns. Also: whistleblowing vs. disparagement.

---

### 32. Don't wait in ambush

**Gloss:** Don't hold a grievance waiting for the perfect moment to strike back. Let it go before it becomes a trap.

**Core tension:** The patience that is actually resentment. The long game of revenge.

**Themes:** ambush, patient revenge, grievance, waiting, long-term resentment, political vendetta, cold anger

**Search seeds:** "vendetta" "long-term grievance" "political revenge" "waiting for the moment" "cold anger" "patient resentment" "ambush" "delayed retaliation"

**Notes:** Political stories about long-held grievances finally acted upon; legal vendettas; the patience of those planning payback. Also: the psychological cost of maintaining resentment.

---

### 33. Don't bring things to a painful point

**Gloss:** Don't use your words as weapons — especially against vulnerable people. Don't make a point at someone's expense, even if you can.

**Core tension:** The power to wound held in check. Restraint at the moment of maximum possible damage.

**Themes:** restraint, power, weaponized language, vulnerability, the point you didn't make, mercy

**Search seeds:** "restraint" "held back" "didn't say" "mercy" "power" "weaponized" "could have but didn't" "discretion" "mercy"

**Notes:** Stories about people in power who chose not to use it — journalists who didn't publish, prosecutors who didn't charge, leaders who chose de-escalation. The exercise of power through restraint.

**Search seeds:** "burden" "transfer" "offload" "externalize costs" "who pays" "environmental justice" "labor" "exploitation" "power asymmetry" "cost-shifting"

### 35. Don't try to be the fastest

**Gloss:** Don't compete spiritually. Don't try to win. Practice isn't a race and the person who looks most advanced is often most confused.

**Core tension:** The competitive instinct applied to something competition destroys.

**Themes:** competition, optimization, speed, winning, metrics, productivity culture, hustle culture, measurement of unmeasurable things

**Search seeds:** "optimization" "hustle culture" "productivity" "competition" "fastest" "winner" "metrics" "quantified self" "race to the top" "efficiency"

### 36. Don't act with a twist

**Themes:** hidden agenda, transactional giving, strings attached, gift with strings, altruism research, performative virtue, conditional kindness

**Themes:** zero-sum, others' suffering, schadenfreude, exploitation, comparative happiness, success at expense of others

**Search seeds:** "schadenfreude" "zero-sum" "success at expense of" "exploitation" "comparative" "others' suffering" "structural advantage" "privilege" "comparative wellbeing"

**Notes:** Strong candidate. Stories about industries built on others' pain (predatory lending, private prisons, pharmaceutical pricing), comparative happiness research, schadenfreude research.

## Point Seven: Guidelines of Mind Training

*The final set — practical guidelines for keeping the practice alive in daily life.*

---

### 39. All activities should be done with one intention

**Gloss:** Whatever you're doing, let the underlying intention be compassion — the welfare of others. One root intention unifies all activity.

**Core tension:** Many actions, one intention. The simplicity beneath complexity.

**Themes:** intention, unified purpose, single motive beneath many acts, simplicity, underlying motivation

**Search seeds:** "single purpose" "unified" "intention" "motivation" "underlying" "root cause" "simple beneath complex" "one thing" "core value"

**Notes:** Stories about individuals or organizations with unusually clear and consistent purpose — or conversely, those who discover their many activities actually serve one hidden intention.

---

### 40. Correct all wrongs with one intention

**Gloss:** When things go wrong — in your life, in the world — bring one intention to it: may this be workable for all beings. Not solving the problem, but meeting it with that orientation.

**Core tension:** The single orientation that doesn't fix anything but changes everything.

**Themes:** response to wrong, orientation, meeting difficulty, intention over outcome, equanimity in crisis

**Search seeds:** "response to crisis" "orientation" "meeting difficulty" "how we respond" "intention" "crisis response" "equanimity" "orientation under pressure"

**Notes:** Similar to 39. May pair with it. Best angle: stories about how individuals or communities orient in the face of catastrophe — not what they do but how they hold themselves.

### 41. Two activities: one at the beginning, one at the end

**Gloss:** Begin each day with intention (what am I doing this for?) and end each day with review (how did it go?). Simple bookend practice.

**Core tension:** The bracket around the day that makes the day a practice rather than just events.

**Themes:** ritual, beginning and ending, daily practice, intention, review, structure, rhythm

**Search seeds:** "ritual" "daily practice" "morning routine" "evening review" "beginning and ending" "structure" "rhythm" "intention setting" "reflection"

**Notes:** Strong angle: research on morning/evening routines, the science of reflection, how structured beginnings and endings change outcomes. Also: stories about people who maintained simple daily practices through extraordinary circumstances.

### 42. Whichever of the two occurs, be patient

**Gloss:** Whether things are going very well or very badly, practice patience in both. Good fortune is not an excuse to stop practicing; bad fortune is not a reason to collapse.

**Core tension:** Patience as an equal response to extremes. The practice that holds in both directions.

**Themes:** patience, extremes, good and bad fortune, equanimity, not being swept away, stability

**Search seeds:** "patience" "equanimity" "extremes" "stability" "not swept away" "both directions" "good and bad" "resilience" "consistency"

**Notes:** Stories about people who maintained equanimity through both success and failure — especially those whose response to good fortune was as steady as their response to bad.

### 43. Observe these two, even at the risk of your life

**Gloss:** Keep two commitments above all: your general commitments to ethical behavior, and your specific commitments from practice. These are non-negotiable.

**Core tension:** The line you will not cross. The commitment that holds even at cost.

**Themes:** non-negotiable, commitment, ethics, the line, what we won't do, integrity under pressure, cost of commitment

### 44. Train in the three difficulties

**Gloss:** Three hard things: recognizing a negative emotion as it arises (not in retrospect); interrupting it in the moment; not returning to it. These are the three difficulties of practice.

**Core tension:** The gap between knowing something is harmful and actually stopping in the moment.

**Themes:** recognition, interruption, not returning, habitual pattern, impulse control, emotion regulation, in the moment

**Search seeds:** "impulse control" "emotion regulation" "in the moment" "pattern interruption" "habit" "recognition" "behavioral loop" "breaking the cycle" "response vs. reaction"

**Notes:** Neuroscience of emotion regulation, research on impulse control, stories about people who broke destructive patterns. The three-stage structure maps well onto clinical research.

### 45. Take on the three principal causes

**Gloss:** Three things make practice possible: a good teacher, conducive circumstances, and personal merit (virtue you've accumulated). Cultivate and appreciate these conditions.

**Core tension:** The conditions you didn't create for yourself that make your growth possible.

**Themes:** conditions, gratitude, infrastructure, what enabled you, teacher, circumstances, inherited advantage

**Notes:** Stories about unacknowledged conditions of success — the invisible infrastructure, the teacher who changed a life, the circumstance that made the breakthrough possible.

### 46. Pay heed that the three never wane

**Gloss:** Keep three things constant: gratitude to your teacher, appreciation of the teachings, and commitment to practice. These should not diminish over time.

**Core tension:** Gratitude as a practice that requires maintenance — it erodes without effort.

**Themes:** gratitude, maintenance, erosion, taking for granted, constancy, what we forget to value

### 47. Keep the three inseparable

**Gloss:** Body, speech, and mind should all be engaged in practice — no separation between how you think, what you say, and what you do.

**Core tension:** The gap between inner and outer, between what we believe and how we act.

**Themes:** integrity, coherence, mind-body-speech unity, gap between belief and action, embodiment, alignment

### 48. Train without bias in all areas; it is crucial always to do this pervasively and wholeheartedly

**Gloss:** Don't practice compassion selectively. Your practice should extend to everyone — those you find easy, those you find difficult, strangers, enemies.

**Core tension:** The selectivity of compassion as its failure. Real compassion is unconditional or it's not compassion.

**Themes:** unconditional, selectivity, bias, partial compassion, inclusion, who we exclude, universal vs. conditional

**Search seeds:** "selective compassion" "who we exclude" "bias" "unconditional" "universal" "partial" "in-group compassion" "empathy gap" "conditional kindness"

**Notes:** Research on empathy gaps — we feel more for those similar to us, for individuals vs. statistics, for nearby vs. distant suffering. Stories about the limits of compassion as practiced.

### 49. Always meditate on whatever provokes resentment

**Gloss:** The thing that triggers you most is your most important teacher. Don't avoid it — turn toward it deliberately.

**Themes:** resentment, irritant, turning toward, trigger, enemy as teacher, provocation, shadow

### 50. Don't be swayed by external circumstances

**Gloss:** Your practice doesn't depend on conditions being right. Don't be blown about by circumstances — good news, bad news, praise, blame. Stay grounded.

**Core tension:** The stability that doesn't require stable circumstances.

**Themes:** groundedness, stability, conditions, external circumstances, not swayed, equanimity, stability in chaos

### 51. This time, practice the main points

**Gloss:** Don't get lost in peripheral details. This life, this moment — practice what actually matters: compassion, clear seeing, helping others.

### 52. Don't misinterpret

**Gloss:** Six things commonly misinterpreted: patience (waiting until conditions are favorable, not genuine patience), yearning (for worldly things, not for awakening), excitement (about entertainment, not practice), compassion (for those you like, not everyone), priorities (worldly gain over genuine development), joy (at enemies' misfortune).

**Core tension:** The way a virtue's outer form can disguise its opposite.

**Themes:** misinterpretation, false virtue, appearance vs. substance, masquerade, fake patience, conditional compassion, selective kindness

**Search seeds:** "false virtue" "masquerade" "conditional" "selective" "appearance vs. substance" "misinterpretation" "patience as waiting for payback" "compassion for some not all"

**Notes:** Excellent candidate for a more complex found poem. The six-fold structure gives shape. Stories where the language of virtue is deployed to describe its opposite — "patience" in political contexts meaning waiting to retaliate, "compassion" that only extends to in-group.
